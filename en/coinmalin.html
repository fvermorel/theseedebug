Crete

Crete is the biggest island in the Aegean Sea. At the epoch of King Minos, it was far more powerful than the small cities of the continent which usually make war between them. Today it is inhabited by about 600,000 people who live partly on agriculture and partly on tourism: its beaches are among the most beautiful of the Mediterranean.

Cnossos

It was in Cnossos that king Minos reigned. Today, from his palace, only impressive ruins remain and are visited by dozens of thousands of tourists every year. If you go there, tell yourself it was there that Theseus and Adriane met ... more than three thousand years ago!

Athens

For a long time Athens was a Greek city of a small size before it grew in power and became the first city to adopt a democratic functioning. In the 5th century before Jesus Christ, it would be the dwelling place for the most illustrious Philosophers: Socrates, Plato, Aristotle… Today Athens is the capital city of Greece and has more than three million inhabitants.

Along the centuries

The stories of Minos and Theseus have inspired a great deal of artists all along the centuries. Here are some of the most remarkable ones:

-460. This vase represents the fight between Theseus and the Minotaur. Ceramics is an art extremely developed in Ancient Greece and many museums have very important collections of them.

-340. This representation of the Minotaur and his mother, Pasiphae, is an evidence of the ancient artists’ sense of humor.

Between 1520 and 1523 « Adriane in Naxos », by Titian. This painting is conserved at the national Gallery in London. Adriane has a movement of fright in front of the cortège of Dionysus and the countless creatures following him. But the look of the god who flies in the air is anything but threatening.

1700-1710 : Bacchius and Adriane, oil painting by Sebastino Ricci, conserved at the national Gallery in London. This time, the artist represented Adriane asleep and Dionysus coming to her as if in a dream.

Around 1638, Theseus found his father’s sword, by Nicolas Poussin, oil on canvas, conserved at Condé Museum in Chantilly, in France. As much as the character it is the monumental setting that has interested the painter.

1826. Theseus fighting against the Minotaur, sculpture by Etienne-Jules Ramey, 1826, conserved in the Tuileries Garden in Paris in France. Theseus is represented as a warrior triumphing over a slaughtered monster.

1636, Rubens : « Icarus’s fall », conserved at the Royal Museum of Fine Arts in Belgium. The scene is all the more tragic because Daedalus and Icarus are on the same level and the father cannot do anything to save his son.

1943. « Icarus’s fall » by Henri Matisse, belongs to a particular collection. There, it is a cosmic representation of Icarus as a poetic figure who wanted to touch the stars.

1937. « The Minotaur carrying a horse » by Pablo Picasso. For the artist, the Minotaur represents the uncontrollable violence. Picasso develops the figure of the horse in other paintings, and more particularly in one of his greatest masterpieces: ”Guernica”

1960. Theseus and the Minotaur (Teseo contro il minotauro), Italian peplum by Silvio Amadio. The story of Theseus has certainly interested the cinema. Silvio Amadio’s film, alas, is no longer well-known.

2005. Screenshot of a tauren in the video game « World of Warcraft » by Blizzard studio. Video games have also got nurtured with the ancient Greek imaginary. Here, the taurens constitute one of the races players can play in the course of their adventures.

The flying machines

Like Icarus, man has always wanted to fly like birds. Of course, it was considered for a long time as an impossible dream.

Leonardo da Vinci’s booknotes

In the 16th century, Leonardo da Vinci drew plans of flying machines. They would never function as they had no engines. However, their plans enormously resemble the planes we know.

©Wikipedia

The Balloons

The first flying machine was created by Frenchmen: the Montgolfier brothers, Joseph and Etienne, in the 18th century. Those brilliant engineers had the idea of filling a balloon with warm air, which enabled a basket with its passengers to rise up in the air.

The first airplanes (1/2)

The first man to make a vehicle heavier than air take off was Clément Ader, a French engineer. In 1890, he launched the first plane in the world, Eole, from the name of the god of wind. His machine had the appearance of a bat, and was not easy to handle and so was abandoned later on.

The first airplanes (2/2)

Orville and Wilbur, two Americans, perfected the first airplane: contrary to Ader’s plane, it was possible to turn and maneuver the plane. In 1904, they carried out the first flight in close circuit. In 1908, the pilot Louis Bleriot was the first man to cross the English Channel through the air.

©Wikipedia

Military aviation

Planes got perfected during the First World War (1914-1918). They were first used to spy enemies, and then they got armed. It was the epoch of the “Aces” who competed of virtuosity. During the Second World War (1939-1945), aviation would play a more preponderant role and would be even more perfected.

©Wikipedia

Jet aircrafts

After the Second World War, civil aviation developed: first for the transportation of merchandise, then, more and more, for the one of passengers. In 1952 the first jet was displayed: its speed reached 800km/h. The Concorde, at the end of the 60s, would be the first civil jet to exceed the wall sound.

Moon rocket

However man does not stop there: like Icarus he seeks to go always higher. In 1957, the Russians launched the first satellite inhabited by a living creature: the Sputnik. In 1969, the American Neil Armstrong was the first man to walk on the moon. From then on, leads and robots have been sent to all the solar system planets… while waiting to go even farther?

Democracy

The Athenian democracy

The Athenian democracy is considered as the most ancient in the world. The citizens organized meetings and voted in a show of hands. They were selected by drawing lots. However this democracy is very imperfect: neither women nor slaves, of course, could vote.

The Pnyx, where athenian citizens used to debate

The Roman republic

Born in -509, the Roman republic is not really fairer: all the power was in the hands of the Senate and it was very difficult for ordinary men from the people to become senators. In -31 the empire replaced the republic.

Screenshot of Spartacus by Kubrick + caption

The English Parliamentarian democracy

In the 17th century, in England, the Parliament took the power. It was a non-violent revolution. A king or a queen is still on the throne but he or she cannot declare war without the Parliament’s consent. It is also the Parliament that decides on laws and guarantees the respect of freedoms.

the American Parliamentarian democracy

In the 18th century America was a colony dominated by England. After the Independence War, the United States was founded: today it is the oldest republic in the world with a president elected by the people. The first president of the United States was Georges Washington.

Portrait of George Washington by Gilbert Stuart Williamstown, 1846, source : Wikipedia

The French revolution

In 1789, the French revolution was first directed against the nobility more than against the king. But the latter betrayed the people and was finally executed. The republic was declared in 1792. Its motto: ““Liberté, égalité, fraternité” (: Liberty, equality, brotherhood)

Oath of palm game, David, 1790

The women’s vote

For a very long time, women did not have the same rights as men. The Napoleon code treated them as eternal minors who pass from the father’s guardianship to the husband’s. The first country to grant them the voting right was New Zealand in 1893. Then followed the other countries: The United Kingdom, Russia and Canada in 1918, The United States in 1920 ... and France, only in 1945!
