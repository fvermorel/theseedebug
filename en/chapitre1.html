The king of Crete
The king of Crete ⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁

It was the epoch of king Minos, the son of Zeus, and the mortal Europa. He reigned over the whole isle of Crete, but his power stretched even farther: all the Greek cities paid him a tribute. For many years he was married to Pasiphae who bore him a lot of children. As he was strict but fair, his subjects feared and respected him, and he neither favored his friends nor overpowered his adversaries. Thus, under his reign, the kingdom of Crete reached a splendor that had never before been attained.

At that time, Greece was not a unified country. Every island, every town, had its king. And among them all, Crete was the greatest and the most powerful of the nations.
A bull rose out of the sea ⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁
The first injustice the king committed would cause him great suffering. Poseidon, the king of the sea, made a magnificent white bull rise from the sea-foam, and ordered Minos to sacrifice it to him. Dazzled by the splendor of the animal, he could not resign himself to do so, and substituted another animal. Alas, the son of Zeus should have known that the gods could not be deceived so easily! Poseidon’s revenge would be terrible and would not take long to come.

A child unlike the others
⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁
Poseidon made the queen Pasiphae fall in love with the white bull: since Minos had preferred the animal to the god of the sea, his wife would prefer the bull to him. Nine months later, the queen gave birth to a very strange baby boy: a human body, but a bull head, and human hands, but hooves instead of feet. It was the Minotaur. As a crowning misfortune, despite all his mother’s love, there was nothing human about the child. When he grew up, he became a furious beast hungry for blood!
The Minotaur was not the only creature half-man and half-animal: the harpies are women-birds, the sirens women-fish, and the centaurs men-horses.
At the end of the labyrinth
⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁⠁
At Minos’s court lived the greatest inventor and architect in Greece: Daedalus, who, for many years, had searched for the means that would enable man to fly. Overwhelmed by shame, the king asked him for help. The minotaur, the shame of the king and of Queen Pasiphae, had to be hidden from the people. Daedalus built a labyrinth, a prison made of interminable corridors and with such a complicated floor plan that, once inside, it was no longer possible to find the exit. The Minotaur was thrown there. Thus would the dark secret of the royal couple be concealed from everybody’s sight.
